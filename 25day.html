The 25 Day Challenge

Inspired by Jennifer Dewalt’s 6 month learning sprint, and subsequently Thuongvu Ho‘s 100 day challenge, I started my own month (or rather, 25 day) long challenge to learn Django, by making one Django web app a day.

Immersion is best when you’re learning something new. Apparently, if John Grisham has done his research, the way the Powers-that-Be train monolingual secret agents to become efficient undercover spies with flawless accents, is by dumping them in the middle of the foreign country and abandoning them for a few months. Hey, nobody said being in the secret service was easy. And when their exile is over, I assume the spies emerge holding a martini and throwing around idiomatic phrases.

All of which goes to prove that there’s a lot to immersion learning. It’s a pity we don’t really get opportunities to do more of it in school or college.

I’d decided to learn to code this year. Not in an theoretical way where I can solve problems that Codeacademy gives me, but to be actually able to build things- web apps with Django in particular. I want to be at least good enough to be able to rapidly develop a prototype or MVP of any idea I have.

The language I initially settled on was Python, a choice I’ve never had occasion to regret. I like Python– it’s clear, readable, and feels intuitive in many ways. Also, I knew that eventually I’d want to work with law and language, and Python is the ideal choice for that. When I started the project, I’d already worked my way through Codeacademy’s Python track, Zed Shaw’s Learning Python the Hard Way and the University of Reddit course, most of Tango with Django, and attempted the Django Polls tutorial, so I wasn’t a complete beginner. I was very new to Django, however.

Initial Frustrations

I postponed starting the challenge till I knew my way around the basics of Django, which was a good decision in retrospect, considering it took me a ridiculous period of time — nearly a fortnight, before I successfully deployed my first “Hello World” app. Setting up the environment drove me up the wall, and when I found out my computer didn’t even support virtualization, I had to swap it for another one. When I saw the first “Hello World” text – strangely bare, devoid of CSS, it still felt like a huge challenge overcome.

Visible Improvement is exciting

The best, and the most exciting part of doing a challenge like this — you can see yourself getting better, right before your eyes!

Learnings

Finish fast

The project forced me to have something ready every day – and to finish something every day. That was a great thing – finishing something is important, much more so than beginning.

Failing is okay

Many of my apps are failures, at least to my eyes. You’ll find me whining about them in the posts. Of course, many of the apps will have bugs, and some look downright terrible. That’s okay. This challenge was all about speed, accelerated learning, and you can’t spend time adjusting text shadows and playing with css.

This is a story I found on Thuongvu Ho’s blog, which touched a chord.

A ceramics teacher announced on opening day that he was dividing the class into two groups. All those on the left side of the studio, he said, would be graded solely on the quantity of work they produced, all those on the right solely on its quality. His procedure was simple: on the final day of class he would bring in his bathroom scales and weigh the work of the “quantity” group: 50 pounds of pots rated an “A”, 40 pounds a “B”, and so on. Those being graded on “quality”, however, needed to produce only one pot — albeit a perfect one — to get an “A”. Well, came grading time and a curious fact emerged: the works of highest quality were all produced by the group being graded for quantity. It seems that while the “quantity” group was busily churning out piles of work—and learning from their mistakes — the “quality” group had sat theorizing about perfection, and in the end had little more to show for their efforts than grandiose theories and a pile of dead clay.

Instant Gratification can be a good thing

The Django tutorial leaps to mind. It’s a good tutorial, and when I came back to it in the middle of the project to build the Believe it Or Not app, I found it very useful. But all I wanted to do when I’d just started learning was to see something up there, just a Hello World, and not endlessly fiddle with the admin layout. When you’re still swimming in Part 2 of a seemingly never-ending, complicated (for your level) tutorial, it’s easy to give up. Instead, it’s best to start small, finish doing something, and use that momentum to move on to more complicated projects.

Result

So, 25 apps made in 25 days. I finished with the confidence that I’d be able to quickly prototype any app I wanted to make. I wouldn’t be completely lost. Even if I didn’t know exactly what to do, I had experience in searching for answers, applying different solutions to problems, solving problems as soon as possible and moving on. I had experience in shipping an app. I’d figure it out.

And here are the apps.

Day 25: Yoda- Converts your text into Yoda-speak.

Day 24: Netflix Search- Search the Netflix database for movies.

Day 23: Clickbait- Type in subjects you want to write about and get clickbaity blog titles.

Day 22: Bread of Life- Type in any kind of food to see tasty pictures and links to recipes from hundreds of cooking blogs.

Day 21: Rock Paper Scissors- Play Rock-Paper-Scissors with the computer.

Day 20: Spirals Dashboard - Personal dashboard that updates achievements in real time, by gathering data from various APIs of apps such as Beeminder and Google Drive.

Day 19: Sentiment Analysis - Enter your text, or submit a URL. The app parses it and tells you if your text is positive or negative, and provides a positivity score.

Day 18: Resume Builder - Enter your details, and download a nicely formatted pdf resume.

Day 17: Believe it or Not- Quiz app – asks you random multiple choice questions

Day 16: Happy - Log in with Twitter and make a rating of how happy you feel right now, and your rating is added to a dynamic, interactive chart with everyone else’s. Built with Highcharts.

Day 15: Smarter than You - An app that converts your text into “smart speech”

Day 14: Text Detective A simple text analyzer built with NLTK that guesses the subject of your text.

Day 13: Somerset- A tool to make your writing simple, effective, and clear. Highlights passive voice, adjectives, sentence length, lexical diversity, repetitive words, and encourages simple word choices.

Day 12: Charged- A to-do app; you can make an account, add, delete, edit and mark to-dos as done

Day 11: Enlightenment- An app that gives you random, meaningless statements for your enlightenment.

Day 10: Make Better - Barebones Hacker News clone

Day 9: Secret Lists- An app to write secret lists of things, built to use user permissions

Day 8: MyPasteBin- Pastebin app – a unique url is generated for you which you can share

Day 7: TIL - Gives you interesting, random facts, but only if you contribute a fact yourself.

Day 6: Pet Forum- An app meant to find someone to take care of your pet, featuring mainly user profiles

Day 5: Valentine- An app that lets you input a name, and generates a Valentine’s greeting for them

Day 4: Foodie- An app that grabs data from the Google Maps and Locu APIs to display restaurant details from any city around the world

Day 3: Mixter- An app to share cocktail recipes, with user registration made necessary to post cocktail recipes

Day 2: Confessions- An app that lets you input your confession and displays it along with those of others

Day 1: Landing Page - A super simple landing page that saves a visitor’s email and redirects them to a thank you page
